The best path is to build a **low-maintenance discovery system** that compounds
on its own:

**1) Search discovery** Publish pages that are easy to crawl, index, and answer
from.

**2) Owned distribution** Use an email list and RSS so readers can come back
without you “showing up” on social platforms.

**3) Evergreen topic coverage** Write tightly related posts that reinforce each
other over time instead of chasing trends.

That is the closest thing to “discoverable without social media” that also
preserves work-life balance. Google says most pages are found automatically
through crawling, and there is no payment or manual submission shortcut that
guarantees ranking. It also says existing SEO best practices remain the way into
AI search features. ([Google for Developers][1])

What I would do in your position:

First, make the site technically boring and reliable. Have clean HTML, crawlable
pages, no accidental `noindex`, a sitemap, and Search Console/Bing Webmaster
Tools set up so you can catch indexing problems early. Google’s docs say
sitemaps help search engines understand your URLs and updates, and Bing’s
webmaster guidelines cover discovery across Bing search experiences as well.
([Google for Developers][2])

Second, lean hard into **query-led evergreen content**. For your niche, that
means posts like:

- “How to design a codebase for AI coding agents”
- “What makes software architecture legible to coding agents?”
- “Vertical slices vs layered architecture for AI-assisted development”
- “How to reduce blast radius in AI-generated code changes”

Google’s current guidance for AI search is to create unique, non-commodity
content that satisfies specific user needs, especially as queries get longer and
more specific. That fits your topic very well. ([Google for Developers][3])

Third, build **topic clusters**, not isolated posts. One strong article helps;
five interlinked articles around the same concept are much better. Search
engines use links and page context to understand a site, and clear internal
linking helps users and crawlers find related content. ([Google for
Developers][1])

Fourth, add **owned audience channels** that do not require daily presence:

- email newsletter
- RSS feed
- optional “new posts” digest once or twice a month

This is not a search ranking trick; it is a compounding attention asset. Google
Search Central itself still offers RSS for updates, which is a good signal that
feed-based publishing remains a practical low-overhead channel. ([Google for
Developers][4])

Fifth, make every article easy for both search engines and answer engines to
extract from. The version we just revised is moving in the right direction:
explicit answer block, question-shaped headings, concise FAQ, and clear
terminology. Google’s AI features guidance says important content should be
accessible in text and that normal SEO practices still apply. ([Google for
Developers][5])

Sixth, avoid the trap of “content velocity.” You do **not** need to post
constantly. For a niche technical site, one excellent article every few weeks
that targets durable questions is usually better than trying to feed social
platforms. Google’s guidance emphasizes helpful, satisfying, original content
over mass-produced material, and says appropriate use of AI is fine as long as
it is not primarily for manipulating rankings. ([Google for Developers][6])

My practical recommendation is this operating model:

- Publish **1 flagship article per month**
- Publish **1 supporting article** or glossary/explainer entry tied to it
- Update older articles when you learn something new
- Keep sitemap/Search Console/Bing Webmaster Tools healthy
- Send **one short email digest** when something new is out
- Skip daily posting entirely

That gives you a discovery engine without turning your evenings into promotion
work. This is an inference from the search guidance plus your goals, but it is
the most sustainable setup for a solo technical publisher. ([Google for
Developers][1])

If you want the single highest-return non-social tactic, it is this:

**Turn the site into the best answer source for a narrow set of recurring
questions.**

Not “write great content” in the abstract. Specifically:

- choose 10–20 recurring questions in your niche
- publish the clearest answer page for each
- interlink them
- keep them updated
- collect email/RSS subscribers from those pages

That is much more compatible with work-life balance than trying to manufacture
attention on Reddit or X.

A few things I would not spend much time on:

- chasing viral traffic
- posting everywhere manually
- trying to “hack ChatGPT rankings”
- pumping out thin articles
- obsessing over schema beyond the basics

Google’s own guidance is pretty consistent here: there is no separate magic
formula for AI visibility; strong fundamentals and genuinely useful content are
still the path. ([Google for Developers][5])

My suggested priority order for you:

1. Make sure the site is crawlable and indexed.
2. Publish a small cluster of evergreen, question-led posts.
3. Add newsletter signup and RSS.
4. Revisit and improve old posts quarterly.
5. Ignore most social media.
